### LESSON 1: What Is Governance Architecture? (30 min)

**VIDEO SCRIPT OUTLINE (15 min):**
- **Hook (30 sec):** "Your company's governance architecture is like your operating system. You don't see it. But when it breaks, everything breaks. Today we're going to make the invisible visible."
- **Section 1 (4 min):** Define governance architecture. NOT compliance. NOT organizational chart. It's the system that determines: who decides what, how information flows, how decisions get made, and how fast the organization can act. Use the "operating system" metaphor throughout.
- **Section 2 (4 min):** Why governance matters in cross-border context. Data: 70% of cross-border leadership failures trace back to governance gaps (not talent gaps, not strategy gaps — governance gaps). Three Forces context: China's operating model going global means governance architectures built for domestic operations are under stress.
- **Section 3 (3 min):** What good governance looks like. Not perfect governance — effective governance. High-performing cross-border organizations share 5 traits: decision clarity, information quality, localization balance, board alignment, execution speed.
- **Visual Notes:** Slide 1: Title with LYC branding. Slide 2: "Governance = Operating System" visual. Slide 3: Data chart — governance failure attribution. Slide 4: 5 traits of effective governance (radar chart). Slide 5: "Your governance is working... until it's not" — closing frame.

**READING MATERIAL (1200 words):**
- **Title:** "Governance Architecture: The Invisible Infrastructure of Leadership Decisions"
- **Section 1:** What governance architecture is (and isn't). Distinguish from compliance, org charts, corporate governance codes. Governance architecture = the actual system of decision-making, information flow, and accountability in your organization.
- **Section 2:** The 5 dimensions of governance health: Board Readiness, Intelligence, Decision-rights, Governance structures, Execution. Brief definition of each. How they interact.
- **Section 3:** Why cross-border governance is harder. Jurisdictional complexity, cultural expectations, time zone friction, regulatory divergence. Data points.
- **Section 4:** Self-reflection: 5 questions to assess your governance health (preview of BRIDGE).
- **Include:** 2 data callouts, 1 anonymized mini-case (4-5 sentences), table of 5 dimensions with definitions.

**EXERCISE (10 min):**
- **Governance Quick Check:** Rate your organization on 5 statements (1-5 scale):
  1. "Our board has the information it needs to make good decisions."
  2. "Local markets have the same facts as HQ."
  3. "Everyone knows who decides what."
  4. "Our committees actually make decisions, not just discuss."
  5. "Decisions get implemented as intended."
- **Reflection:** "Which dimension scored lowest? Why do you think that is?"

**QUIZ (5 questions):**
1. What is governance architecture? (a) Compliance rules (b) The system of decision-making, information flow, and accountability ✓ (c) The organizational chart (d) Board meeting minutes
2. Which of the following is NOT one of the 5 dimensions of governance health? (a) Board Readiness (b) Revenue Architecture ✓ (c) Decision-rights (d) Execution
3. What percentage of cross-border leadership failures trace to governance gaps? Answer: 70%
4. True or False: Good governance means perfect governance. Answer: False. It means effective governance.
5. Short answer: In 2-3 sentences, describe a governance challenge you've experienced.

### LESSON 2: The 5 Dimensions of Governance Health (45 min)

**VIDEO SCRIPT OUTLINE (20 min):**
- **Hook (30 sec):** "Most leaders think they know their governance health. The data says they're wrong. Today, we go deep on the 5 dimensions — and you'll see exactly where your organization stands."
- **Sections (2-4 min each):** One section per BRIDGE dimension:
  - **Board Readiness (B×0.20):** What it measures. What high/low looks like. Common gap: boards designed for domestic operations governing cross-border complexity. Example.
  - **Intelligence (R×0.15):** Information quality, information asymmetry. Common gap: HQ doesn't have the same market facts as local. Example.
  - **Decision-rights (I×0.15):** Clarity of authority. Common gap: RACI exists on paper but doesn't match reality. Example.
  - **Governance (D×0.20):** Committee structures, information flow. Common gap: too many committees, none making real decisions. Example.
  - **Execution (G×0.15):** Speed-to-decision, implementation follow-through. Common gap: decisions made but not implemented. Example.
- **Visual Notes:** One slide per dimension. Radar chart showing hypothetical score profile. Color coding: Green ≥70, Amber 40-69, Red <40.

**READING MATERIAL (1800 words):**
- Deep dive into each dimension. For each: formal definition, what high/low scores mean in practice, 2-3 common patterns, diagnostic indicator (how to tell if this dimension is weak), and what good looks like.
- Include: Dimension weight table. Score interpretation guide. Mini-case per dimension (anonymized, 3-4 sentences each).
- Conclude with: "The composite score matters more than any single dimension. A 60 across all dimensions is healthier than a 90 in one and 30 in another."

**EXERCISE (15 min):**
- **Dimension Self-Assessment:** For each of the 5 dimensions, rate your organization (1-10 scale) and write one sentence explaining your rating.
- **Pattern Matching:** Read 5 scenarios (provided). Match each scenario to the weakest dimension. (e.g., "Your local team made a decision that HQ overruled 2 weeks later because they didn't have the full context." → Intelligence gap.)
- **Reflection:** "If you could improve ONE dimension this quarter, which would it be and why?"

**QUIZ (8 questions):**
- 5 multiple choice (one per dimension — scenario-based)
- 2 short answer ("Describe a situation where Board Readiness was the bottleneck" + "What's the difference between Decision-rights and Governance dimensions?")
- 1 calculation: "If scores are B=65, R=45, I=70, D=55, G=50, what is the composite score?" (Answer: (65×0.20)+(45×0.15)+(70×0.15)+(55×0.20)+(50×0.15) = 13+6.75+10.5+11+7.5 = 58.75)
